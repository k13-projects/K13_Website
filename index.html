01 Who you'd be working with

I don't think of myself as an agency. I'm one builder who takes a handful of projects a year and treats each like it has my name on it — because it does.

I'm Kazim K., founder of K13 Software Studio. I've spent the last years shipping the kind of software that's easy to demo and hard to fake: a real-time AI meeting copilot used in fifteen languages, restaurant-group brand sites that go live on real domains, food-hall platforms running on a CMS, an odds engine that has to be correct or it's worthless.

I work the whole stack — product thinking, design, engineering, the unglamorous launch details — so nothing gets lost in a handoff. Edisyn

Product · Engineering · Design · Web + desktop

Everyone is brilliant one hour after the meeting ends. I built Edisyn because I wanted that hour to happen inside the room.

It listens to your call in real time, tells you who's speaking, hears a question forming, and quietly hands you the answer — and the next question — before the pause gets awkward. Only you see it. What started as a transcript panel became a full product: profiles, summaries, billing, a prompt lab, and a desktop overlay that works over any call.

The part I'm proudest of

One router stands in front of five AI providers and picks per request by cost and latency — and a nightly Playwright suite interrogates production while I sleep. Trust isn't promised here; it's engineered.

Station8

Next.js 15 · Sanity CMS · Mapbox · 508 / axe in CI

Twenty thousand square feet at a university isn't a restaurant — it's a public square. So I built it the way you build a public square: to be owned, entered, and to outlive me.

Every decision answers one question — who does this belong to? It belongs to the team, so Sanity CMS lets them rewrite everything without calling me. It belongs to everyone, so it's 508-compliant and axe-tested in CI. It even belongs to the building: the map wears a custom Mapbox style mixed to its olive and green-glass.

A scar I left in the log on purpose

One night a security header silently strangled every animation. The commit that brought it back is titled, plainly, "Unbreak the homepage." Real work has scars; I don't hide them.

Miramar

HTML/CSS/JS · GitHub Actions · client reporting

Some clients hand you a brief. This one handed me a building from 1938 — and the job of being its first impression in a new century.

The Miramar Theatre, restored as a food hall. Its Spanish-Colonial bones gave me the teal, cream and burnt orange; the marquee returns as pixels, bulbs and all, with an animated Coming Soon stamp that slams across the hero while the kitchen crews move in. A landmark deserves a drumroll, not a placeholder.

The move clients rarely get

Every month, stakeholders receive progress as a single HTML file — images embedded, Gmail-safe, forwardable to any partner in one click. Trust isn't a deliverable; it's a rhythm.

CENGO

HTML/CSS/JS · YouTube API · Google Sheets

An artist shouldn't need a developer to update their tour dates. So I gave CENGO a site that updates itself.

Underground, after-dark, high-contrast — built to feel like the music. It pulls live YouTube channel stats and the latest drops automatically, and the entire events calendar runs off a Google Sheet the artist edits like a spreadsheet. No CMS to learn, no backend to babysit.

The trick under the hood

A Google Sheet as a CMS, the YouTube API as a live feed, and a graceful fallback if either hiccups. Zero backend, zero excuses — the cheapest infrastructure that still feels alive.
