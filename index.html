Plate I Edisyn · 2025—pp. 04–11
I.

The meeting,
answered.

MediumWeb + desktop product
RoleProduct · Eng · Design
MaterialsReact, Supabase, Deepgram, 5 LLMs, Stripe
StatusLive — edisyn.ai ↗
Provenance1,122 commits & counting
Printed on the client's stock

Everyone I know is brilliant one hour after the meeting ends. I built Edisyn because I believe that hour belongs inside the room.

It began with a question I couldn't put down: what if the answer arrived while the question was still hanging in the air? So I taught a page to listen. Live speech-to-text that knows who is speaking. A copilot that hears a question form and drafts the response before the silence gets awkward. Profiles, summaries, billing, an admin panel — and a prompt lab, where the AI rehearses against mock meetings before it is ever trusted with a real one. Then a desktop app, because confidence has to live where the meetings live.¹

Every choice underneath serves one conviction: trust is engineered, not promised. Five AI providers stand behind one router that picks per request — because no single model deserves blind faith.² The night Stripe said “paid” while the app said “free tier,” I stayed in it until the webhook told the truth. The fix shipped. So did the nightly Playwright suite that interrogates production while I sleep³ — because at 9 a.m., people walk into rooms with my software open and something real at stake.

Plate II Tiger Hospitality · 2025–26pp. 12–19
II.

A family of brands,
one address.

MediumBrand site · maps · motion
MaterialsVanilla JS, Leaflet, GitHub Pages
StatusLive ↗
Provenance21 release branches
Printed on the client's stock

A parent brand has exactly one job: make every child look like the favorite. Tiger Hospitality needed a single address that raised all of its names at once.

I dressed the group in black and gold deliberately — a house brand should be the velvet in the case, not the loudest jewel inside it. The tiger reveals itself once, in the loading screen, then steps back and lets the concepts take the light. The heart of the site is an interactive map where every location opens with its brands' logos standing side by side,¹ because this family's true story is geography: every pin is a bet the group has placed on a neighborhood, and the map lets you watch the bets multiply.

Hospitality is the art of letting people in — so even the vendor pitch became an invitation: a card that flips over to reveal the email, like a door quietly opened.² Twenty-one release branches later, this is the group's front door. When Oceanside opened, the pin was on the map before the ribbon was cut.³

Plate III Miramar Food Hall · 2025–26pp. 20–27
III.

A landmark,
reopened.

MediumSite · brand · client reporting
MaterialsHTML/CSS/JS, GitHub Actions
StatusLive ↗ · venue opening
ProvenanceEst. 1938 · San Clemente
Printed on the client's stock

Some clients hand you a brief. This one handed me a building from 1938 — and the responsibility of being its first impression in a new century.

The Miramar Theatre showed films before most of us were born; I was not going to wrap it in a template. The teal, the cream, the burnt orange — they come from its Spanish Colonial bones. The marquee returns as pixels, bulbs and all. And across the hero, an animated Coming Soon stamp slams down¹ — because anticipation is part of hospitality, and a landmark deserves a drumroll, not a placeholder.

The move I'm proudest of is one visitors never see: every month, stakeholders receive the project's progress as a single HTML file — images embedded, Gmail-safe, forwardable to any partner with one click.² Because trust is not a deliverable; it is a rhythm. And the site was security-hardened before the first stakeholder ever touched it³ — a first impression is also a promise.

Plate IV Station8 Public Market · 2026pp. 28–35
IV.

Industrial bones,
warm light.

MediumNext-gen site · CMS · maps
MaterialsNext.js 15, Tailwind v4, GSAP, Sanity, Mapbox
StatusPre-launch hardened
Provenance20,000 sq ft · La Jolla, CA
Printed on the client's stock

Twenty thousand square feet at a university is not a restaurant — it is a public square. So I built the site the way you build a public square: to be owned, to be entered, to outlive its maker.

Every decision answers one question: who does this belong to? It belongs to the team — so Sanity CMS lets them rewrite vendors, events and every line of copy without ever calling me; a site you cannot edit is a site you rent. It belongs to everyone — so it is 508-compliant and axe-tested in CI,¹ because a public market that turns anyone away at the door has already failed. It even belongs to the building: the map wears a custom Mapbox style mixed to its olive and green-glass, down to the last hex.

One night the homepage went silent — a security header was quietly strangling every animation. The commit that brought it back is titled, plainly, “Unbreak the homepage.”² I left that title in the log on purpose. The work is real, and real work has scars.

Plate V TrustMeBro · 2026pp. 36–43
V.

Show
the math.

MediumProduct · odds engine · UI
MaterialsNext.js, TypeScript, EV engine
StatusPrivate beta
Provenance48 pull requests of probability
Printed on the client's stock

The name is a joke. The ledger is not. I built TrustMeBro to see what happens when bravado is finally forced to show its math.

Every group chat has the friend with a lock of the week. I gave him an expected-value engine and, more dangerously, a public record: picks scored in the open on the Bro Board, every coupon tracked leg by leg, win by win, void by void.¹ Forty-eight pull requests of probability and edge cases — including an alt-line engine that waits, patiently, for its live June trial.² The interface never flatters a bad pick. That honesty is the design.

The decision I would defend in court: when a game is cancelled mid-coupon, the engine does not lazily refund the ticket. It re-prices the remaining legs — exactly as the real sportsbooks do.³ No user asked for that. That is precisely the point. Integrity is what you build when only the code is watching.

Plate VI baa atelier · 2026pp. 44–51
VI.

Quiet luxury,
loud craft.

MediumEditorial site · art direction
MaterialsNext.js 15, Motion, Lenis, Resend
StatusShipped
ProvenanceFor Baha Irs's studio
Printed on the client's stock

Baha Irs paints surfaces the way other people write memoirs. Her site had to whisper — and a whisper, it turns out, is the hardest voice to engineer.

I built it like a magazine because her work belongs in one: Playfair italics, paper and sage and clay, headlines that unmask themselves as you arrive, scroll that moves like a page being turned.¹ The discipline was restraint. Every pixel had to earn its quiet; every animation had to justify its existence to the work it framed.² Maximalism is easy — you just keep adding. Reduction is a negotiation with everything you love.

And because beauty without business is just decoration, the most honest commit in the repository reads: “Make the site sell, not just show.”³ That sentence outgrew the project. It is now the bar every K13 work must clear — including the monograph you are holding.
